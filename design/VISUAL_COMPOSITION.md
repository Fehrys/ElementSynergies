# Visual Composition

## Purpose

This document defines how every game screen should be composed.

Composition is more important than decoration.

A beautiful composition with placeholder art is preferable to beautiful assets inside a poor layout.

Every future screen should follow these principles.

---

# The Screen Is A Place

The player should never feel like they are looking at software.

The player should feel like they are looking through a window into a living miniature world.

The interface exists inside that world.

Not above it.

---

# Depth

Every screen should contain multiple depth layers.

Foreground

Interactive Layer

Characters

Enemies

Environment

Background

Lighting

Atmosphere

Nothing should appear completely flat.

Objects may overlap naturally.

---

# Layer Philosophy

Foreground

Decorative objects may slightly overlap the puzzle board.

Examples:

- herbs
- kitchen towels
- wooden spoons
- leaves
- flour

These objects should increase immersion without blocking gameplay.

---

Interactive Layer

The puzzle board is always perfectly readable.

Nothing important may cover puzzle tiles.

Gameplay clarity is sacred.

---

Character Layer

Heroes live directly behind the puzzle board.

They are part of the environment.

Not UI.

---

Enemy Layer

The monster occupies its own visual space.

Large monsters may partially overlap background decorations.

Never overlap puzzle gameplay.

---

Environment Layer

The world surrounds gameplay.

Shelves.

Lanterns.

Kitchen furniture.

Smoke.

Plants.

Barrels.

These create the illusion of a real location.

---

Background Layer

Large shapes only.

Walls.

Windows.

Cave openings.

Sky.

Never detailed enough to distract gameplay.

---

Atmosphere Layer

Light rays.

Dust.

Steam.

Floating embers.

Tiny particles.

Very subtle.

Always moving slowly.

---

# Visual Density

Avoid empty space.

But also avoid clutter.

Every area of the screen should answer:

"What story does this object tell?"

Decorative elements should reinforce fantasy without competing for attention.

---

# Composition Hierarchy

The player's attention naturally follows:

1 Monster

↓

2 Puzzle Board

↓

3 Heroes

↓

4 Effects

↓

5 Interface

Nothing should compete with the puzzle board while dragging.

---

# Shapes

Avoid large rectangles.

Prefer organic silhouettes.

Wood bends.

Paper curls.

Cloth folds.

Rope hangs.

Kitchen tools overlap.

Nothing should feel generated by software.

---

# Controlled Asymmetry

Perfect symmetry feels artificial.

Slight asymmetry creates life.

Examples:

One lantern hangs lower.

A towel is folded differently.

One shelf contains more ingredients.

One knife is slightly rotated.

These imperfections make the world believable.

---

# Material Storytelling

Every visible material should communicate warmth.

Wood

Copper

Iron

Ceramic

Leather

Paper

Fabric

Stone

Each material should have visible age and use.

Knife marks.

Burn marks.

Scratches.

Steam.

Flour.

Grease.

Nothing feels factory-made.

---

# Camera

The camera looks slightly downward.

Like a player standing above a kitchen workstation.

Avoid completely flat orthographic compositions.

A subtle perspective creates depth.

---

# Golden Rule

When removing all interface elements, the remaining image should still feel like a beautiful fantasy illustration.

The gameplay should appear to exist naturally inside that illustration.
